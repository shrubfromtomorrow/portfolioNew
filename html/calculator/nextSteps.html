The Vision
So hopefully you are reading this after having had a look at the rest of the timeline though I wouldn't blame you if you skipped it, it takes a while to read. I wanted to, at the end of all of this, reassert what I am trying to do here. The end goal is to have students that need to take the SAT or the ACT (or any other standardized test for that matter) look on Amazon or in Best Buy and right next to the $150 graphing calculators, see a $50 dollar option with as many necessary features and good reviews as all of the big brands. It's a tough task, but even as I head into the next stage of my life with university, it's a challenge I am going to continue to pursue, all because a calculator cost too much.

You thought that was a conclusion huh? No, I have a bit more to say.
Moving Forward
There are a few things that I want to mention regarding the future, some are core aspects of the project, some are just in regards to the physical design of the calculator.
The first thing I want to talk about is the next stage of prototyping.

The single biggest drawback to my previous prototype is my choice of compute unit. Using a Raspberry Pi is fundamentally against the vision of this project as it is an incredibly open piece of hardware. Not only does it hold a full OS that bad actors could get into, the whole point of something like a Raspberry Pi is that you have control over your hardware, something that would not lend to approval for use in schools and testing environments. Here we get the stage of the project that I am currently working on.
In late February (2023) I met with Dan Rosenstein whose current title is "Group Product Manager - Advanced Autonomy and Applied Robotics at Microsoft". He has an incredible amount of experience with embedded systems and would be a great help with the next stage of this project. During our meeting we talked about the long term goals of the project and the general pathway to turning something like this into a product. One of the ideas I took away from the conversation was the suggestion to create a prototype of the calculator with an Arduino. Arduinos are an MCU (microcontroller rather than the MPU that a Raspberry Pi is (microprocessor)) and do not have an operating system built in, they run firmware directly through a bootloader. This would be fantastic in terms of locking down what the calculator could possibly do: as long as people cannot upload their own firmware to the Arduino, it will only ever do one thing. The other great thing about an Arduino is the form factor. The Arduino I am currently developing with as my main processor is the same size as the Arduino I used for the keyboard in the Raspberry Pi prototype: far, far smaller than the Raspberry Pi I used before. Most Arduinos also do not have internet access capabilities anywhere in their hardware, another plus.

It isn't all sunshine and rainbows however. Arduinos are weaker in the CPU department, the one I use has a clock speed of 16mhz compared to the 1.4ghz of the Raspberry Pi. This doesn't mean that my Arduino will calculate things as slowly as a TI calculator would, it just means that it is slower than my previous prototype. There is also the issue of memory. The Arduino I use only has 32kb of flash memory. Fitting the same capabilities as my previous calculator onto 32kb is going to be tough. Another drawback is that I won't be able to develop software the same way as I did before. There is no such thing as a web browser, HTML, CSS, or even JS in an Arduino; everything that I write will have to be in Arduino's C++ dialect. Development would take a long time, but it isn't the main idea of this next prototype. This prototype is physical: how can I make this calculator exactly what I want from a hardware point of view? Once I prove that I can put a keyboard, battery, screen, and compute unit all in a decent form factor, that is when I will search for funding and create my own MCU with more memory and a faster CPU that fits my form factor exactly.

So far in my Arduino journey, I have gotten to the stage where I can input numbers from my keyboard and display them on a display. That seems rudimentary but it really isn't. It took about 2 months to get to this point. I had to get a screen that works with my Arduion, wire it up correctly using its 8-bit parallel interface (no such thing as HDMI in Arduino), find a library that could communicate with an 8-bit parallel interface (most display libraries for Arduino are built for a serial interface like SPI), write my own key matrix code (QMK firmware exists to output to USB, something that I don't have on Arduino), and lastly parse all of those individual voltages from the keyboard into numbers to call functions to display stuff on my screen. It is hard to develop something so complex on an Arduino, but it has been fun.
I am not going to detail the entire development process thus far as in depth as I did for the Raspberry Pi prototype as I want the 20-20 hindsight when I write about it, and I am still in the midst of developing it.

The other piece of the future I want to talk about is in regards to a core change in this project. A couple weeks after my meeting with Dan, I had a meeting with an old advisor named Ben. He teaches math at Issaquah High School and I thought that getting the input of an actual math teacher would be a good idea when it came to making good decisions with this project, after all, they are the ones that will allow or disallow a certain calculator in their classroom, I need to sate them.
Ben gave me some valuable insight into how delicate the balancing act of adding features can be. Certain grade levels need to prove that they can actually perform the menial math task (say understanding the general shapes of graphed functions) before they can have the luxury of being able to get the answer quickly from a calculator. This also ties into what I will call the generationality of calculators. The generationality of calculators comes from needing several calculators throughout a student's life. Plenty of students get basic arithmetic once in 5th grade, a scientific calculator once in 6th grade, and a graphing calculator once in 9th grade. That's a lot of calculators, it would be nice to just give a 5th grader a graphing calculator and have them not need the more advanced functionality until they are older. But students cannot be trusted to have that level of power, if you need a middle school student to find the y-intercept of a linear function, you want to make sure that they can do the algebraic work before allowing them to just use the more advanced functionality to calculate the intercept. Ben and I were discussing this when I had the idea of a multi-module calculator: what if students bought one compute unit (with a screen and battery) and as they leveled up their education, they would be allowed to use more different keyboards that enabled greater functionality. Basically, a student's parent buys them a calculator in 5th grade and they also buy the arithmetic keyboard. The compute unit still has the ability to graph functions and do things like matrix multiplication, the student just can't access that functionality without having a keyboard that can send the right input to the compute unit. As the student goes into middle school, high school, or even university, rather than buying a new calculator each time, the student/parent only has to buy another keyboard module. Teachers can verify that a student has the correct module installed with a quick glance and can be assured that the student is doing the mental work where they need to.

This has a few benefits: lower cost over a student's lifetime, and less electronic waste. When a student is done needing their basic arithmetic module, it can just be passed on to another student for free or sold on Ebay, further reducing waste. It also would save districts buying in bulk a lot of money: if this hypothetical calculator corporation just needed to manufacture one type of compute module, their manufacturing costs go down and they can pass the savings onto districts.
I think this is a fantastic idea, though I am a bit biased, and it wouldn't necessarily be that difficult to develop. I think that more than anything else I will do, this will be the most disruptive to the market.

And that is all I have to say. I am hopeful for the future of this project and I hope it makes as much of a splash as I want it to.
