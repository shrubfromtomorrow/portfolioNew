Intro
This is the capstone project for my senior year at Gibson Ek.

I found myself in October of 2021, frustrated with the price of a graphing calculator. I needed to buy a graphing calculator for my upcoming ACT and I wanted to get a decent one. I found the average cost for a calculator with a decent resolution for graphing and a color display was around $110.

I found myself outraged, and even more so after I got my TI 84 Plus CE and found it to be slow and clunky. Buying a graphing calculator is limited. There are only a few reputable brands with only a few halfway decent models worth buying. Ignoring the sponsored links, the results on Amazon for "graphing calculator" consist of 8 Texas Instruments devices and one Casio (at the time of writing).

With whole tablets costing $50 dollars to manufacture, surely there is a way to create a calculator that cost very little, is far more powerful than other brands, and is usable on standardized tests.
Right?
Research
Any good in-depth project has got to start with some research. I started having a look around for root causes in the price of graphing calculators (keep in mind that I didn't know how I wanted to tackle this issue at first).

The first main article I looked at was made by the Washington Post (this link does require a subscription, try putting the link into 12ft.io if you want to remove the paywall ;)
This article might be old, but it is still relevent. It highlights the massive strides that have been made in technology in the two decades previous and how graphing calculators do not reflect that same leap in technology.

The article also talks about how Texas Instruments had (as of 2014) accounted for 93% of all graphing calculator sales, Casio taking the other seven percent up to a useful precision.

By far the most important thing to take away from this article is this line: "“Compared to other electronics this day and age there is very little content,” said Barclays analyst Blayne Curtis of the TI-84 Plus. “Plastic case, small black and white screen, two semiconductor chips. The batteries are even not rechargeable like a cell phone.” He estimates a TI-84 Plus costs $15-20 to manufacture and has a profit margin of over 50 percent for Texas Instruments."

This article goes much further in depth than I wish to here and I recommend reading it to gain an idea of the frustration I feel over this topic.
